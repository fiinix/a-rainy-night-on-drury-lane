Preface

I never intended to write a novella, it just—sort of—happened. Back when I penned Part I, which would later be titled With Sensual Red Lips, I never meant for it to be more than a one-off, self-indulgent collection of literary cliches.

But then my wife asked, “What happened next?”

To which I, of course, had no reply because it had never crossed my mind that it would go anywhere at all.

And now, rather than to tell you this beautiful tale about how she had stirred something deep inside me, I will instead tell you the real reason I continued with this story. The real reason that a short one-off grew from its humble origin of a 1 000 words into the—still quite humble—10 000 word novella you now have before you.

Sure, the real reason isn’t nearly as complex or nuanced as a blatant lie would be. In fact, I think you’ll find that my reason is quite mundane, but at least it’s mine.

 

I, simply, wanted to please her curiosity.

 

Now, to those of you who have already read this story, there isn’t really any reason for you to read it again, you know how the story goes; Boy takes one last job to make rent, boy dies at the end.

But if you’ll indulge me one more time, I think I can make it worth your while. Because, although the main story has remained intact, a smaller—more personal—story emerged. One that I hadn’t anticipated. And rather than try to fight it, I gave in to it and made it a part of the original story.

Finally by going through and editing, I could tidy up all those loose ends I had left behind and structure what had lacked a lot of focus in its infancy. And I think it’s better for it. I hope you’ll feel the same way.

Carlos Eriksson

Maidstone, United Kingdom

Prologue

That White Blinding Light

“Then the witches rushed through the air with great cries,” Catherine threw her hands into the air, “and fled back to Slievenamon, uttering strange curses on the Spirit of the Well, who had wished their ruin. But the woman and the house were left in peace and a mantle dropped by one of the witches in her flight was kept hung up by the mistress in memory of that night; and this mantle was kept by the same family from generation to generation for five hundred years after.”

“The end.”

Kissing Henry and Mary on their foreheads Catherine tucked them in a little tighter than she normally would, “That was the last one. No more stories tonight.”

“But mam please—” they both cried out in unison.

“Not tonight. Now it’s bedtime for you two snappers,” blowing out the candle she left the door ajar to let some light in from the kitchen.

Stepping outside and inhaling the crisp night air was just the kind of wake up she needed. The night was deceitfully calm. Two months ago President Thomas Woodrow Wilson had asked congress to declare war on the German Empire, for the good of the American people and “To make the world safe for democracy,” as he so idealistically had put it. The European War had already been raging on for almost three years now and Catherine had already lost enough in a war she didn’t believe in.

She, like so many other Irish immigrants, thought that the British Empire deserved no help from America but with no position to voice her thoughts, she kept them to herself.

Walking towards Patrick’s Ale House, Catherine tried to not let her nerves get the best of her. Summer was racing towards them this year and she already found it difficult to move around during the day because of the heat. But she also knew better than to complain though, as any ‘real’ New-Yorker would instantly remind her of a heatwave a few years back, “Whose like can never be contested.”

So she tried to enjoy the cool night air but found herself too distracted by a million little thoughts, all which wanted her attention.

Stepping into the tavern the smoke and smell of an evening’s worth of cigarettes took her by surprise and she found herself struggling to breath through the thick wall of smoke.

The alcohol was flowing freely and Catherine could hear a delightful fiddling tune—that instantly reminded her of home—in the background. Even so, she didn’t want to spend a minute more than necessary in this place.

“Excuse me, where can I find a man named Rory?” Catherine carefully asked the woman behind the bar disk.

“That’ll be ‘im, right over ‘dere,” the woman replied as she pointed towards an unkempt man engrossed in a game of Darts he was only observing, with one hand stroking his beard and the other hand safely rested on a pint of ale.

“Are you… Rory?”

“I may or may not be, love. I take it your not a guard by the looks ‘o ‘ya,” the burly man in front of her never dropped his eyes of the game of Darts.

“Whadda want? I’ve got no bloody time for culchies,” trying to get something out of his teeth Rory’s tongue was moving around methodically.

“Maggie sent me. She said you would have some—” she took a deep breath, “—work for me.”

“A culchie like you? Don’t make me laugh,” glancing at her he continued, “Besides, our kind ain’t too popular anyway and that scar of yours ain’t doin’ ‘ya no favours.”

Catherine instinctively reached for her sore black eye, the sudden touch reminded her of that white blinding light she had seen as her landlord’s open palm had hit her face.

“Ask me bollocks, you’ve got ‘til the end ‘o the week to pay rent,” her landlord had said without flinching.

But she knew all too well that Rory had meant the scar along the left hand side of her face. The permanent reminder, unlike her black eye which would fade with time, of that unfortunate accident from Catherine’s childhood where she and her brothers had played carelessly in a field where some farmers had forgotten their tools and she had tripped and fallen face first on a hoe.

Stunned but equally relieved at her rejection Catherine mustered her courage and asked, “Well, is there anything else I could do? I have two children and—”

“Yeah, yeah,” Rory’s attention was back at the game of Darts, “With yer face I might ‘ave somethin’ you could do.”

“Anything.”

“That’s the spirit, love. ‘Ave ‘ya heard of the Gopher Gang?” Rory asked and drank the rest of his pint.

“That’s… that gang on Hudson Street isn’t it?”

“Close enough. It just so happens that I’ve got myself a bit of a problem with one ‘o the lads in what’s left of the Gophers. I was going to take care of him myself but somethin’ tells me this would be the perfect way to see if ‘ya’ve got the stomach for what comes next. I’ll also pay ‘ya $160 to remove this problem from my life,” without a drink in his hand, Rory was back to trying to dislodge whatever was stuck in his teeth.

“Comes next?” she whispered to herself.

“What’s it gonna be? Like I said, I’ve got no bloody time for culchies. You think you can stomach somethin’ other than chicken blood on those pretty little hands of—”

“I can,” Catherine said with as much strength as she could gather.

“Good. The man you’re looking for goes by the name of Sidney ‘Smallfists’ McCoy, a good for nothin’ thug who usually hangs around Seventh Avenue. Come back here and we’ll talk more when he’s not running his gob anymore,” Rory started rolling himself a cigarette.

Catherine, seeing this as her opportunity, got up to leave. The thought of staying another second made her stomach turn and she needed to get out into the fresh night air again.

“I never did catch your name, now did I?” taking his eyes of the game for the second time Rory watched Catherine walking away.

She paused only long enough to answer him, “Just call me Grace.”

I

With Sensual Red Lips

The rain is pouring down on Drury Lane, covering the city of Chicago and filtering out the wailing sirens with its white noise. Detective Wentworth is sitting at his desk, listening to his favorite tune by Marion Harris, a half-smoked cigarette in one hand and a glass of scotch—the cheapest his bootlegger had—in the other.

He is only a dame with a revealing short dress away from being a symptom of his time and he knows it. He knows it too well. As he takes another sip of his scotch and fights the urge to vomit—he isn’t much of a drinker but feels obligated to be—the harsh clicking sounds of a woman’s high heels begin to echo through the hallway of his office-turned-apartment.

After deciding to go into early retirement his rent has been overdue for the past three months and whilst he has done his best to avoid his landlord, he knows it’s only a matter of time before Mr. Clay’s greedy eyes finds him, sneaking in late at night to avoid a confrontation. He preferred to not think about what would happen to him, should Mr. Clay ever find out that he didn’t have any money. The subdued clicking sounds of heels against damp wooden floorboards grows closer.

“Maybe I thould try to make a run for it? Thhe ith probably nothing but trouble,” he thought to himself.

Detective Wentworth had long ago come to terms with his—sometimes incomprehensible—lisp. This was the way he had always spoken and no amount of ‘corrections’ from his father would ever change that, but what he found difficult to understand was, “Why did his thoughts also have a lisp?”

He had made some—mostly failed—attempts at training his lisp away in his teens but with very little results to show for it, he had decided that they were doomed to fail, and since then, tried his best to forget all about them.

Detective Wentworth could now hear how the dame—who he imagined was blonde with sensual red lips and breasts held tightly by the dress she wore so shamelessly—just outside his door. Not expecting visitors—he never had any—Detective Wentworth had felt no need to put on pants this morning. A decision he now regretted, as he realized she would knock on his door any second.

Three faint knocks and then, nothing.

“Juth a thecond, I’m a bit buthy,” having found his pants, he was about to get his fly closed when the threading in the last button came undone and the button rolled underneath his desk.

The three knocks had decreased to two.

“Please, may I come in? I need your help,” the desperation in her voice was unmistakeable.

Sitting down at his desk again to conceal his otherwise visible underpants Detective Wentworth replied, “Yeth, of courthe. Come in.”

Despite being at least 20 years older then Detective Wentworth, he couldn’t help but find himself thinking she was quite the looker. Expect for the gray streaks that had begun to cover her once golden blonde hair she was exactly as he had imagined. Her lips were painted red and the dress she wore, with no shame in sight, held her breasts in tightly.

Gesturing her towards the only vacant chair in the room he continued, “Thit down. Would you like a glath of thcotthh?”

“Thcotthh?” she said looking questioning at him.

Lifting his glass and looking towards the bottle on his desk he tried again, “Can I get you a drink?”

“Oh, no thank you. I don’t drink anymore,” folding the dress underneath her as she sat down, “Though at times like these I almost wish I still did. You see, my little Bobby didn’t come home for Thanksgiving.”

Her breathy voice was distracting Detective Wentworth from listening with his fullest attention but he managed to pick up enough pieces to form a decent picture of what had happened.

Anna McWilliams, mother of three successful children, one of who was senator Robert ‘Bobby’ McWilliams, would bring her family together every Thanksgiving.

“You might be a big shot senator now but you’re still my little Bobby and I want you home for the holiday,” she would remind him of whenever he tried to make excuses for why he wouldn’t be able to make it.

“Will you make the apple pie like I like it?” Bobby always asked.

To which she would reply, “Have I ever made it any other way?”

“Fine mom, I’ll see at 5pm.”

Except the clock had struck five. And six. And seven. And finally on the stroke of eight, Anna had called senator Robert McWilliams’ assistant Helen who had insisted that, “Senator McWilliams had left his office at 4.15pm, heading towards the family home to celebrate Thanksgiving with his family,” and that she hadn’t heard from him since.

At which point Anna McWilliams had picked up her phone for a second time, called her lawyer and asked for the number to a good private detective. Unfortunately all the good private detectives that Mrs. McWilliams’ lawyer knew were either too preoccupied or too dead, and he had instead given her the number to the first private detective he found through the yellow pages.

A fact that neither Anna McWilliams or Detective Wentworth knew.

“So, can you please find my Bobby?” reaching from her purse she handed him an envelope bulging from its content, “Here’s half and I’ll pay you the other half as soon as you can give me any more information about my Bobby.”

“I’ll thee what I can do. But I won’t make any promitheth yet,” Detective Wentworth had learned the hard way to not make promises unless he was sure he could keep them.

Reaching into her purse again Mrs. McWilliams handed Detective Wentworth an unrecognizable blob made from a thin strip of metal, “My Bobby can get a little suspicious sometimes but if you show him this, he’ll know that I sent you.”

After studying the metal blob for a moment Detective Wentworth realized what he was holding: A gingerbread cookie cutter in the shape of an angel.

“I’ll thee what I can find out. I’ll get back to you in a few days,” Detective Wentworth got up and showed Anna McWilliams to the door.

II

The Brown Note

A few rain soaked hours later Detective Wentworth found himself at the Brown Note, a sleazy jazz joint for con artists, criminals and aspiring musicians, trying to get a drink from a barkeep imaginatively named Joey. After several failed attempts at ordering scotch from Joey, Detective Wentworth had settled for beer.

“Maybe I thhould thtart drinking beer inthtead? Eathier to thtomach and eathier to pronounthe,” he thought to himself as he was finding a vacant table to occupy.

He was waiting for an informant to show up, a shady immigrant with many aliases who had recently taken to calling himself Nobody.

“Who are you talking to?” someone would ask and then receive the cheesy but effective answer of, “Nobody. I’m talking to Nobody.”

It worked 90 percent of the time. The remaining 10 percent was reserved for henchmen with baseball bats and a score to settle. Nobody didn’t have many options in life and had played the cards handed to him. A mouse in a country of cats, he simply made the best of the situation—even though that sometimes meant going head-to-head with the henchmen of whoever he had betrayed—to get whatever he needed.

“Half loaf is better than no loaf,” Nobody would remind himself when times got even tougher, “Half loaf is better…”

Detective Wentworth’s eyes followed the young woman selling cigarettes from her tray—hoping she would spare him a glance—as he was drawing the last breath from the last cigarette from a packet he had bought as recently as last night. It was meant to be his last packet of cigarettes.

Then again, the Sparrow Brothers case was supposed to be his last case and look how that turned out.

“Thingth have a habit of not ending when they’re thuppothed to,” he muttered faintly as the cigarette saleswoman walked by. He gestured towards her and as he caught her attention, she also caught his.

“Jothephine?” he gasped, but as he left the question mark lingering in the air he could tell by the look on her face that she was definitely not Josephine. What he had at first thought to be the spitting image of his Josephine, now only resembled her when looked at from the corner of an eye. It was the way she moved that had deceived him but now that he was looking directly at her, he could see clearly. Swaying back and forth like a drunk—she probably had an inner-ear problem just like Josephine—as she reached him he could see that she looked nothing like her. “Eight more beerth and I would have never known the differenthe,” he thought to himself.

“What’ll it be, sugah?”

Her accent was thick and she made no efforts to hide it.

“Three packetth of Deep Throat and tell Joey I’ll have another beer.”

“Sorr hon, I only gots me two packs of ‘dem Deep Throat left. But I’ll give ‘dem to ya for 23¢”

“Here you go, and you can keep the thhange if you can get that beer here on the double.”

He wasn’t convincing anyone that he was cool but there was no point in stopping his attempts now, “Might ath well go the whole hog,” he thought to himself and realized that he hadn’t heard that expression since his father passed away. “Never start what you can’t finish,” his father would always say before he would hold him across his knees to pass out what he referred to as Patriarchal Justice. Meanwhile his mother would hide in the kitchen baking and pretending that she couldn’t hear the cries coming from the living room.

The memories of his childhood had sent shivers down Detective Wentworth’s spine and his timing could not have been worse. Nobody had entered the Brown Note through a back door and walked unnoticed up to Detective Wentworth just he was reminiscing about his heinous childhood. The already quite jumpy Nobody got even more suspicious as Detective Wentworth’s shivers jogged him out of his memories and back to the cold harsh reality.

“You Mister Wentworth? You don’t look good. And Nobody not telling nothing to somebody who look like you look!” Nobody’s English might have been broken but his message was clear as day.

“Perhapth a beer would inthtil thome more trutht in me?” he hoped Nobody would at least understand the word ‘beer’ in his otherwise incomprehensible choice of words.

“Beer? No. Nobody not drink beer. Beer is for zhenshchiny. How you say, woman-people? No, Nobody want two bottles of vodka and Nobody sing like Crested Lark, yes?”

Detective Wentworth had kept the cookie cutter shape in the left pocket of his overcoat and now he was caressing the cold metallic angel between his fingers to remind him of why he had agreed to take on the case.

The Josephine look-alike made her untimely return with his second beer just as he was about to agree to Nobody’s terms.

“What’th thith? I didn’t order a beer! I only drink thcotthh! Two bottleth of vodka and a glath of your thheapetht thcotthh. And make it quick thweetheart!” he quickly said before she had any time to reply.

Noticeable upset—she was nobody’s sweetheart but her mom’s—she slammed the beer on his table anyway as she turned around and started swaying back and forth back to the bar. Pointing towards Detective Wentworth she yelled toward Joey, “Two b’ttles of Russian water and a glass o’ Horsepiss,” and stormed out through the same back door that Nobody had sneaked in through.

Detective Wentworth knew he was going to have to apologize to her later if he wanted to get a good nights sleep, “Maybe thhe will thettle for an ethplanation and a heartfelt thorry?”

He turned his attention back to Nobody—who by now had calmed down enough to take a seat—and tried his best to look serious as he handed Nobody a worn photograph of a man in his mid-fifties wearing a pinstripe suit.

“Tell me everything you know about thenator McWilliamth and hith dithappearanthe. Why would thomeone want him gone?”

III

Underneath a Turquoise Sky

The first two bullets missed Detective Wentworth by only a couple of inches.

“Thon of a bithh!” he yelled out as he threw himself behind a large dumpster. Another bullet flew by, taking with it a fist sized piece of brick wall as it ricochet into a nearby pile of newspapers.

“Where’th my gun! Where’th my gun?” palming his coat pockets trying to find his Smith & Wesson .38—a panicked thought entered his mind—”Thhit, I forgot it in the office! I took the thtupid cookie cutterth with me but I forgot my revolver.”

Just as Detective Wentworth was about to surrender to the idea that he had forgotten his revolver he could feel the hammer of the .38.

The sense of relief washed over him, like a cold ice cream on a hot summer day, as he found his Smith & Wesson .38 in his inner left pocket. “Tho, today might not be the day I die after all,” he thought to himself as he pulled out the .38 and swung open the cylinder.

“Four. Only four bulletth left. Gotta make ‘em count,” he tried to focus his train of thought before taking aim towards the mysterious shadow in the alley, who only a few seconds earlier had tried to make his brain a permanent part of the wall behind him. Squeezing the trigger he shoots and misses.

“Three.”

“What if there’th more than one gunman?” the thought hadn’t crossed his mind until now. If there were two of them, he was done for. Detective Wentworth wasn’t the sharpest shooter by any standards but he might still get a lucky shot. But he would never get lucky twice.

“Two.”

“Thith ith it. The one time I forget to take thothe ecthtra clipth with me is the thame day I get thhot at.”

The rain had subsided a few minutes earlier and as luck would have it, the sun finally began to peak out behind the dense veil of clouds, letting out a few streaks of light that made their way past him and reflected of the glasses worn by the mysterious shadow in the alley. He would never get a better shot than this and didn’t waste the opportunity.

“One.”

His final shot did as all the other shots before it, and missed.

Click, click.

He threw the .38 to his side as another set of bullets flew through the air like angry wasps, causing a lone pigeon to awaken from its midday slumber and fly away in panic.

There he was, a slightly overweight Private Detective named Dick Wentworth, on what was looking to become his last job. The clouds had dispersed completely now and as he sat crouched underneath a turquoise sky, behind a dumpster—filled with spoiled crayfish and other crustaceans—he couldn’t help but think about some of the choices he had made throughout his life.

A loud twang echoed through the alley as another few rounds ricochets off the dumpster.

Stealing a kiss from Sarah Robinson back when he was in fourth grade, “Worth it.”

Finding his fathers hidden stash of alcohol and consuming it all whilst he was home alone, only to vomit it up 15 minutes later, “Worth it.”

Tricking that poor retarded boy in his boarding school to eat dog shit by convincing him it was pudding, “Worth it.”

Forgetting to take the extra clips for his .38 when he left the office three weeks ago, “Tho thtupid.”

Click, click.

An all too familiar sound, expect it wasn’t Detective Wentworth’s revolver that had made the sound this time.

In the blink of an eye Detective Wentworth jumped out from his protective dumpster and rushed towards the mysterious shadow.

Caught off guard by Detective Wentworth’s energized sprint the shadow blurted, “Scheisse!” before quickly turning around to run away. But the still wet ground made it difficult to get traction and Detective Wentworth caught up with him halfway through the alley tackling him to the ground.

“Verdammtes Schwein!” the mysterious stranger exclaimed as he hit the ground.

Detective Wentworth regained his composure faster than the stranger and as he stood up he did his best to seem threatening and imposing, which was helped by the massive amounts of adrenaline pumping through his veins.

“Who thent you? Who wantth me dead? Give me a name and give it fatht!” he was practically screaming at the stranger who had only just regained his balance and was beginning to stand up.

“Du kannst mich mal am Arsch lecken, Mistkerl! I werde dir nie was sagen—”

The stranger—making no intentions to stop his cursing—was slightly taller than Detective Wentworth but before either of them had the chance to notice it Detective Wentworth punched him in the left eye causing him to go down on one knee.

“Don’t make me have to repeat mythelf! I don’t like repeating mythelf!”

The adrenaline was still pumping, although at a substantially lower level, and he could feel his aggressive demeanor heading towards a humiliating halt if he didn’t get some useful information out of this stranger soon. Left with very few options and vivid flashbacks of his own experiences with school yard bullies he did the first thing that came to mind, and kicked the stranger in the groin.

The crunching sound echoed through the alley and the German’s cursing turned into a wheezy couching.

“Na, ja, ja! Ich spreche doch,” he put his left hand up to suggest defeat—cupping his testicles with his right hand— and tried to stand up again.

“Aber du sollst mich auch nicht wieder treten, du amerikanischer Schweinehund,” he muttered under his breath as he finally stood up.

“My name is Viktor Kuhnert. Ich wurde nicht hierher geschickt um dich zu töten, man wollte nur dass ich dich verschrecken sollte. I have not been sent to kill you, only to scare you away. But seeing as we are both out of bullets, I think it will be better if you come meet meinen Arbeitgeber. Maybe you are a man mit dem man vernünftig reden kann? A man of reason? Here, take my pistol, as you can see ich habe keine Kugeln mehr. No bullets.”

As Viktor Kuhnert leaned forward and handed over his Borchardt C-93 pistol, Detective Wentworth saw a Großkreuz tattoo on his left collarbone.

“Shall we go? Mein Auto steht on the other side of this building,” Viktor tried his best to seem sincere as he gave way for Detective Wentworth.

IV

Leering Pale Green Eyes

Peering through the ominous night sky, the moon is shining down on an undisclosed industrial area. The only sounds heard in the background are the crickets with their lonely tune and some faint indistinct Baroque music playing on a gramophone.

Detective Wentworth is sitting on rickety wooden chair trying his best not to vomit into the burlap sack he’s wearing on his head. The car journey here, he could have handled but being blindfolded with an old burlap sack smelling of figs was more than he could handle. As he forces the nausea back down his throat he can hear a door opening in the distance and synchronized footsteps approaching. But before he has time to speak up, a hand grabs on to the burlap sack and pulls it off his head.

It doesn’t take long for his eyes to adjust as the only two sources of light comes from the moon peering in through the barred up windows and the thin cigarette held between the teeth of the shortest of the five men standing in front of him.

“I apologize for ze unsanitary sack you were forced to wear Herr Wentworth but we were not expecting visitors on such a short notice and I am afraid we are in no position to take risks right now. But I do not wish to seem impolite, can I get you something? You look a little thirsty, some water perhaps?” the short man signals to one of the soldiers as he puffs to inhale another breath from his cigarette.

“Jawohl, Herr Oberstleutnant König!” springing to attention the soldier steps forward.

Lieutenant Colonel Walter König’s tone of voice turns harsher as he addresses the soldier, “Bringen Sie uns Wasser und zwei Gläser. Wir wollen doch nicht dass Herr Wentworth uns für Barbaren hält.”

Eager to impress his commanding officer the soldier is only gone for a few minutes before returning with a canteen of water and two glasses.

“We need you to not find Senator Robert McWilliams. And as mein Mitarbeiter Herr Kuhnert already tried and failed to ‘persuade’ you using ze violence, I would like to appeal to your intelligence.”

As Detective Wentworth looked at Viktor Kuhnert, who was standing a bit further back, he could now see the similarities between Viktor and himself. A slightly overweight man in in his mid-forties, dark brown hair, leering pale green eyes framed by thin glasses. Whilst Detective Wentworth had no need for glasses, they both had forgettable faces.

“I’m afraid time is no longer on our side so I am going to get to ze point. There is an ambitious and dangerous man in Deutsches Reich who is trying to start a revolution. Ze men I work for, and their Triumvirate, has already stopped him once but ze volk has begun sympathizing with this common Gefreiter and his crusade for Lebensraum. Senator McWilliams ‘shares’ our nationalistic views and he is vital in our plans to permanently remove this Gefreiter.”

“So, Herr Wentworth, what will it take to get you to stay out of our plans? Money? Women? Men?”

For once in his life Detective Wentworth had managed to stay focused on the talkative man in front of him, and had heard every word of the surprisingly good English he had spoken.

“Let me get thith thtraight, you’ll give me whatever I want and in exthhange I’ll jutht forget I ever heard of a man named Thenator Robert McWilliamth?” he made his best effort to conceal the excitement over this prospective deal.

He was quite tired of trying to find this elusive Senator anyway and given enough money he could retire, again. And stay retired.

Whilst Detective Wentworth took careful sips from his water glass—fighting the lingering nausea from the fig smelling bag—he daydreamed about all the things he could buy with this imaginary money of his.

He had been eyeing the new Chevrolet Superior Series B that had come out earlier that year, “I could even get two of them and never have to look at my banged up Tin Litthie again”.

And, instead of getting the cheapest scotch his local bootlegger usually had, he would get the third most expensive one.

As Detective Wentworth had gotten older his imagination had shriveled—however small it had been to begin with—even his wildest dreams were now small and ordinary.

“I want 200,000 in American Dollarth—” interrupting himself he then continued, “and I want to meet Marion Harrith”.

Lt. Colonel König pulled up a chair in front of Detective Wentworth and lit himself another cigarette, offering one to Detective Wentworth as well.

“Yeth, thank you. What brand ith thith?” having said his price out loud has given him enough confidence to try some small talk.

“Früher Tod, a very common German brand.”

“Now, Herr Wentworth, I do not know who this Frau Harris is but I am sure we can arrange for you two to meet… and ze money, will not be a problem at all. In fact, as a sign of good faith we will give you half now. So, we have an arrangement, ja? You will give us all ze information of Senator McWilliams and then this meeting never happened.”

Lt. Colonel König signals to the eager soldier again and continues, “Rufen Sie Staatskommissar von Kahr an und sagen Sie ihm, er soll eine Geldüberweisung ausstellen, der Amerikanser ist bereit mit uns zusammen zu arbeiten.”

“Do you have all ze information with you?” Lt. Colonel König says as he turns around to face Detective Wentworth.

“Most of them yeth, and a few fileth at my office. But I can burn thothe,” realizing the ludicrousness of his proposal, he is about to continue when Lt. Colonel König puts up his hand in a stopping motion.

“I am afraid that will not do Herr Wentworth, I must see that we get everything. We do not want to risk leaving anything loose. Me and meine Mitarbeiter will take you to your office to get ze files, you will get ze first half of ze money and we will leave. We will call you to arrange ze rest of the arrangement, ja?”

“Okay.”

“Now, I’m afraid we are going to have to put ze sack on your head again.”

Nodding in compliance, Detective Wentworth finished his cigarette and took a deep breath just as the sack was put back on his head. The pungent smell of figs made him nauseous again.

“Find your happy plathe, find your happy plathe…” he repeated to himself as he fought the urge to vomit again.

V

Nothing But Yellow Faded Newspapers

Once Lt. Colonel König and his conscripts had collected all the files they wanted and left, Detective Wentworth found himself alone in his office again, but this time with a bearer bond for $100,000 lying on his desk and a bright future ahead of him.

First he contemplated taking the next flight to South America but after realizing he didn’t speak the language—come to think of it, he wasn’t even sure what language they spoke—he decided it was in everyone’s best interest—not to mention, his own—to avoid South America for the time being.

Left with few options and as shriveling as his imagination was, he decided to simply go across the corner to his favorite watering hole, the Wench & Hare Inn where he knew most of the local patrons and his cheap no-particular-brand of scotch was usually available. The Inn rarely had any violent crimes so the police left them to mind their own business and would most of the time ignore their violations against the Prohibition Act.

It had started snowing—the first of the winter—on this crisp December morning and with the temperature hovering around 29 °F, this winter was colder than last years. Detective Wentworth hurried across the road to get into the warmth again.

Stepping into the Wench & Hare Inn, Detective Wentworth could hear a song he had heard many times this year but could never name, the lyrics “Trouble, trouble, I've had it all my days” echoing through the half empty Inn and taunting his bad memory.

“Robby, can I get a glathth of your more finer thcotch? None of that cheap thtuff for me tonight. And two packetth of Deep Throat,” he looked around to see who was in this morning.

“Well, if it isn’t another familiar face. Looks like someone’s closed another case, ey? Are you sure about the scotch though, you remember what happened last time? I really can’t afford to upholster the chairs again, Dick,” Robby gave him that same look that his mother use to give him when he had done something stupid with disastrous results.

“I think you’ll find that a lot hath changed thince—”

Detective Wentworth suddenly realized that he hadn’t been at the Wench & Hare Inn since earlier that April.

“—You do know that I haven’t been here thince Arbor Day, right? I think you’ll find that I’ve gotten a lot more tolerant thince then,” he opened one of the packets of Deep Throat and lit himself a cigarette.

“Sure, sure, if you say so. But any repairs are coming out of your pocket this time. And well, the only fancy stuff I’ve got right now is half a bottle of Dickel No. 12. It’s nicely aged though, distilled before they moved their operation to Kentucky,” Robby explained. Of the three things in life he knew well, alcohol was one of them.

“I’ll take the whole bottle. Keep the change jutht in cathe I vomit on anything,” said Detective Wentworth as he placed one of the $50 bills on the counter and went to sit down.

It didn’t take long before he was lost in daydreaming about all the things he would buy for his newly acquired riches. Fantasies about driving Chevrolet Superior Series B began to grow in his mind but before he could rev the engine his thoughts were interrupted by the voice of a woman standing behind him.

“Mind if I join you?” her soft voice was a gentle whisper.

“Of courthe not, have a theat. Are you a thcotch drinker?” he couldn’t do much about the lisp but at least the scotch had done wonders for the stutter he would sometimes get when he got nervous.

A couple of hours later Detective Wentworth found himself back in his run-down office-turned-apartment. But unlike most other occasions he wasn’t alone this time.

“Why don’t you put on some music whilst I pour up the last of this fine scotch?” her name was Grace and she didn’t seem to mind the state of decay that surrounded them.

He drank it all in one sweep—partly to impress her—and partly because to his delight he had discovered that he could handle this scotch better than others.

“It must be such an exciting job to be a private investigator. Tell me, have you ever been shot?” she made no efforts to hide her excitement over the possibility that he had stories of bullets and blood.

“Of courthe I have. It goeth with the job—”

From the corner of his eye Detective Wentworth could—as clear as day—see a shiny white bunny, sitting and eating a piece of carrot. The white bunny was wearing a bright purple tie and a monocle. And as Detective Wentworth focused and tried to decipher whether or not what he saw was real, the bunny looked straight at him and spoke with a gravely voice.

“Hey, buddy! Makes you wish you hadn’t downed it all at once, right?”

Detective Wentworth staggered into a nearby chair, “You drugged me?”

“Poisoned actually. Enough to kill two men of your size, I didn’t want to take my chances in case you would be more resilient. My informant said you wouldn’t be much of a challenge and despite my doubts it would appear he was correct. A pity really, I was almost looking forward to seducing you first but I suppose this will have to do,” the excited glow in her face had turned into a vacant stare.

He should have known better than to think any woman would be interested in a slightly overweight smoker in his mid-forties.

“The Thparrow Brotherth thnt you, didn’t they?” he tried to keep calm but his heart was racing and the bunny was distracting him. Having finished its carrot, it was now preparing to use its own tie as a noose.

“Here, but for the Grace of God. No, the Sparrow Brothers didn’t send me. I don’t know who they are,” she said as she was looking through his wallet trying to find some identification.

“Richard William Wentworth. Your mother didn’t have much of an imagination, did she?”

“If the Thparrow Brotherth didn’t thend you, then who and why?”

“Look, some people really don’t want to be found. My employer just so happens to be one of those people. Don’t take it personally, I’m just doing my job. It was either this or prostitution,” her voice had turned more sympathetic as she was searching his desk drawers for any valuables she could pawn.

“Do you really have nothing but yellow faded newspapers and that dying plant over the—”

“Fig.”

“Excuse me?”

“It’th a weeping fig. A Ficuth benjamina. They were my motherth favorite. Maybe you could give it thome water, I’ve been neglecting it for thome time,” his voice was getting weaker.

As Detective Wentworth’s eyes grew heavier and heavier he could feel his own consciousness slipping away only to rush back as he tried his best to hold onto reality. He tried to steel his mind on the oil lamp burning behind her but it was already too late, his eyes couldn’t focus any more. His heartbeat was beating slower and his mouth was getting dry and cotton-like.

“You guys always have a hidden safe somewhere? Where is it?” she asked and turned around when she didn’t get a reply.

Detective Wentworth’s eyes were now closed.

VI

Dem Dee-Licious Golden Pancakes

“Here’s six bits and a dollar, but please never tell your father I gave you these, okay? You know how he can get,” the concern in his mother’s voice was aimed towards both of them.

“Mom, why would I—”

“Sometimes your father just won’t take ‘No’ for an answer, you know that. We’ll say you earned them doing chores for Mrs. Wilson,” having turned around before she had finished her sentence she was already walking away.

Richard Wentworth stood there for a moment, wishing he could run up to his mother to hug her and tell her that everything would be all right, but he couldn’t. No matter how vivid and tangible all of this felt, it was still a memory and nothing more.

Losing its grip on reality his mind was finding safety in the few ‘good’ memories he had. And in this memory of his, he hadn’t hugged her. Instead he had quickly turned around to run towards to the entrance of the World Fair. In hindsight he wished he had hugged his mother instead.

“That’ll be 25¢, little man,” said the man in the ticket booth.

“I’m actually over fourteen,” replied Richard who was short for his age and was often mistaken for being younger than he really was.

“Well then, little man, who’s actually over fourteen, you get to pay 50¢ like an adult,” the ticket man appeared amused by his insistence on paying full price.

Richard stared in awe at the ticket in his hand. World’s Colombian Exposition it proclaimed proudly and he tried to ignore the thoughts of what his father would do if he ever found out that he had attended the World Fair.

It wasn’t until he reached a sight he had never seen before that he snapped out his trance-like state. Standing in front of him was the 250 feel tall steel construction of the Ferris’ Wheel. The first of its kind. Looking back towards the entrance he could see how the queue slithered all the way up to the man in the ticket booth. He knew he couldn’t be at the fair all day—his father would get suspicious—so he wouldn’t have time to queue for the Ferris’ Wheel but he could at least enjoy the terrifying shadow its steely skeleton cast on the ground.

He continued walking, stopping only to watch an exhibition called Street in Cairo with women dancing and shaking their bellies in a way he had never seen before. Plenty of men and women were standing at this exhibition mesmerized by the exotic dancers but Richard had something else in mind. According to George ‘Doily’ Porter, a German company called Krupp were displaying, “the biggest guns you had ever seen,” somewhere at the fair and Richard was determined to verify Doily’s—often greatly exaggerated—claims with his own eyes.

But as he continued exploring the fair he started losing his sense of direction in the crowds of people and soon found himself in middle of an even larger gathering of people. As he tried to orient himself in the crowd he could hear a powerful voice rise above the tallest of heads.

“Come on folks, don’t be shy. Come closer an’ let good ol’ Aunt Jemima shows you how you too can make dem dee-licious golden pancakes,” said the woman wearing a bandana headband and an apron.

Richard could only see glimpses of her between the shoulders in front of him.

“Only wif my magic recipe can you turn out dem appetitin', taste-delightin' jiffy-quick pancakes that makes ‘yo family happy. An’ you knows what they say; You doubled ‘yo own happiness when ‘yo shared it with dem other folks,” she kept talking whilst stirring the batter, not missing the beat for a second, drawing the crowd ever closer and Richard couldn’t help but get drawn in with everyone else.

“Mm-m-m! My secret recipe is made from wheat, corn an’ rice an’ I’s can guarantee that you ain’t neve’ tasted pancakes so temptilatin’ as these,” she said as she poured up perfectly sized cakes of batter on the griddle.

The sweet aroma of pancakes blew through the crowd, turning an already hungry crowd into a starving one. Everyone was eager to taste Aunt Jemima’s pancakes and the people further back began to push themselves forward in an attempt to better their odds of tasting the first batch.

Special police officers had been assigned to keep the crowds moving but were finding it difficult to remain assertive in the escalating chaos. Richard—figuring he would never get a pancake anyway—took the chance to escape as he saw a small opening to his right.

A few hours later Richard found himself in front of the world’s largest flour barrel again, the lingering scent of pancakes still in the air, but no Aunt Jemima in sight. The previously enormous crowd of a few hundred people had thinned out to a few dozen and most of them seemed to be well fed at this point.

Sitting down on a nearby bench, Richard had to face the facts: Clearly he had no idea how to find the Germans and their big guns and it was getting late. As long as he made it home before 3pm his father wouldn’t see any reason to be suspicious but any later than that and Richard would have to answer questions he didn’t have answers to.

Somewhere along the way—following the directions of a young couple —he had stumbled into the building for Agriculture where he had found an elven-ton block of cheese from Canada and a miniature model of a Western farm but no guns.

“Why honey child, ain’t you the most saddest boy I’s seen today?” Aunt Jemima exclaimed as she came walking out from behind the flour barrel shaped building.

“Yeah, I wath looking for thomething that I couldn’t find,” Richard said with the sound of defeat only a child could. Aunt Jemima sat down beside him and gave him a piece of the pancake she was eating.

“Well, sometimes dem things we be looking fo’, arent’ dem things we be meant to find,” she said and finished her half of the pancake.

Standing back up again, she walked back towards her flour barrel but before closing the door she turned around to leave Richard with another one of her sayings, “You’s know what they say; Dem happy folks don’t measure time by years, but by dem smiles they be spreadin’ in the world.”

VII

A White Blanket of Forgiveness

The snow is falling down on Drury Lane, taking the adulterous city of Chicago and its indiscretions and covering them in a white blanket of forgiveness. The wind is howling through an open window and sirens wail in the distance. Detective Wentworth is slumped in a chair in his office, his eyes are closed and the cigarette is his hand has almost burned to its end.

Grace is standing next to Detective Wentworth. An attractive freckle-faced woman in her late twenties with bright copper colored hair and olive green eyes and with the exception of her two children very few people called her by her real name anymore. “Catherine couldn’t take care of us, but Grace can,” she would remind herself occasionally.

“Bastard.”

“The poison wasn’t supposed to act that quickly,” she muttered to herself whilst throwing Detective Wentworth’s things around.

Holding him by his trench coat she was shaking him violently, “Can you still hear me? Where do you keep the safe?”

His head fell backward as she let go and there was no answer. Pressing her ear to his chest she could still hear his shallow breathing underneath the sound of the skipping record.

“Where have you gone?” she stood up looking at him for a while before turning her attention to the skipping record.

 

…

 

“Sometimes your father just won’t take ‘No’ for an answer, you know that. We’ll say you earned them doing chores for Mrs. Wilson,” having turned around before she had finished her sentence she was already walking away.

Richard Wentworth stood there for a moment, wishing he could run up to his mother to hug her and tell her that everything would be all right.

Every time his mind replayed this moment he would always turn around and run toward the fair, but this time something was different. This time, as if his legs could hear his inner voice, he ran and caught up with his mother, embracing her with his scrawny arms.

“I’m sorry mom, I’m sorry I couldn’t get you away from him in time,” he was struggling to keep the tears away.

Hugging him back she did what any mother would and comforted him, “That’s all right honey, it’s not your fault—”

“But it is,” Richard howled, “If only I had—”

“Pish posh, I chose to stay. To protect you. To keep you safe. That’s what moms are for you know, to protect their little ones. You did all you ever could have,” his mother stroked the hair on his neck as she calmed him down.

Putting her hands on his shoulders she looked him straight in the eyes, “Now, you put those foolish thoughts of yours to rest and go and enjoy yourself, okay? And don’t you worry about me, it’s supposed to be the other way ‘round.”

Richard wiped away the snot on his sleeve and looked back at his mother, “Okay, I will.”

“That’s my boy,” kissing Richard on his forehead she stood up, “I’ll see you later,” his mother said before vanishing into thin air.

 

…

 

Returning from the kitchen—having gone through his cupboards—Grace grabbed Detective Wentworth by the neck of his trench coat again, shaking him backward and forward she tried to wake him up but it was already too late.

Richard William Wentworth, ‘Dick’ to his acquaintances and the few people he had called ‘friends’, had taken his final breath a few minutes earlier. Grace let go when she noticed herself staring into the slightly open mouth of a dead man whose body was still very warm but other than that had no signs of life.

Frustrated at herself for giving Richard too much poison she sat down in the chair by his desk. The armrests were worn out at the edges and the cushion felt like it had no stuffing any more.

“Richard, Richard, where have you hidden these riches of yours?” she said to herself.

There was nothing of any value as far as she could see and most of the junk here looked like the kind you would have to pay someone just to get rid of.

“You didn’t get a lot of visitors here, did you? With the state this place is in, I’m surprised you even invited me back here,” leaning back in the chair she accidentally bumped into a few carefully balanced boxes that proceeded to fall, taking several piles of newspapers with them.

Letting out a loud sigh she crouched down to clean up the mess.

“Wherever I go, there’s always a mess I have to clean up. When is it going to be my turn to sit back and watch as someone else cleans up my mess?”

She looked toward Richard and his flaccid body, slumped down in the rickety chair, “Though I suppose you won’t mind if I tidy this place up a bit, eh?”

Her gaze shifted focus to the bookcase behind Richard and from her crouched down position she would clearly discern what looked like a piece of paper, nestled between ‘This Side of Paradise’ by F. Scott Fitzgerald and a coverless Hemingway’s ‘A Farewell to Arms’.

Richard William Wentworth hadn’t been much of a reader, unless it was horse racing results, but he had enjoyed the look of rows of books and the many adventures he imagined where held between their covers.

Dropping the newspapers from her hands she walked over to the bookcase to discover that what she had mistaken for a piece of paper was actually an envelope. Thumbed and worn on the edges the envelope had the name ‘Richard’ written in beautiful female penmanship on its front.

“Did you have someone special after all?” Grace thought to herself, she only spoke a few words of French—most of which were swearwords—but she could recognize a love-letter when she saw one.

Whoever this Josephine is or was, she had clearly felt strongly about Richard and by the look of the envelope and letter, so had he.

Aside for the letter inside, Grace also found the bearer bond for $100,000.

Grace hadn’t been sure if Richard had been telling the truth when he had bragged about his newly acquired riches. She had told him that he wasn’t the first one to try to get her with a line like that.

Now she stood there silently for—what felt like hours—but was minutes, before finally falling to her knees. Under her breath, as if she still needed to hide it from the world, the tears came streaming down but she was smiling a smile she hadn’t done in years.

“We don’t need you any more, Grace.”

Epilogue

Black And Blue

Of all the detectives east of Missouri, Detective Wentworth had to be the one they had called. The Sparrow Brother’s circus, like all of them, boldly claimed to be the “Most Expectacular Show on Earth” but Detective Wentworth knew better.

More like, “Motht Ethpenthive Thhow on Earth,” he thought.

The early morning air was heavy with the wet smell of hay as the first rays of the sun had yet to warm up the ground. A few locals had gotten up early to watch the circus crew set up their tents in preparation for the evening’s show.

The lions were sleeping and took no notice of Detective Wentworth walking by, unlike the elephant, who seemed eager to make his acquaintance but couldn’t get close enough because of its chained foot.

Detective Wentworth had been told to look for a red wagon in the “back yard” and had been assured that it would be easy to spot once he reached far enough.

“I told you already; I don’t care how bad her knee it, as long as ‘Babyfeet’ is gone Élise needs to stand in her place,” Samuel, the younger Sparrow brother, was shouting and waving his arms around.

Making no efforts to hide his temper Samuel turned to Detective Wentworth, “And the fuck are you?”

“I’m the private detective you hired to find your trapethe girl, Minnie ‘Babyfeet’ Walker,” Detective Wentworth knocked out a cigarette from his crumpled pack of Deep Throat.

“No, you want my brother Arthur. I didn’t hire anyone,” Samuel had started walking away but before he was out of sight Detective Wentworth could hear him shout, “Gillys don’t smoke in our wagons.”

Detective Wentworth slipped the lip-marked cigarette back into its pack.

“Aah. You must be the detective, Mr. Wortwort?”

“Wentworth.”

“Yes, of course, I’m sorry. You’ll have to excuse my poor brother’s demeanor. It’s his turn to be in charge this month and he ain’t too happy about one of our stars disappearing like this. Which is why you’re here. But he ain’t too happy about that either, says your kind ‘brings more trouble than you fix’—” Arthur Sparrow seemed more mature despite being three younger than his brother, Samuel.

Offering Detective Wentworth a cigarette and taking one for himself Arthur continued, “—But regardless of his disposition we need outside help. Our Ms. Walker has been missing for a week and rumors are starting to spread that she has joined another circus.”

“But you don’t think tho?” Detective Wentworth lit the gifted cigarette and thought, “Tasteth like wet horthe. Jutht my luck.”

“Well no. Her ‘father’ Eddie passed away a few years back and even though she has been growing more distant since then, this has always been her home,” Arthur looked tired, like someone who hadn’t had a good nights sleep for months.

“What about her mother? Ith thhe not around?” Detective Wentworth was taking notes on a newspaper he had picked up earlier.

“No, she doesn’t have one. One of our roustabouts found her as a baby behind a bale of hay and ever since that day, Edward ‘Eddie’ Walker—may he rest in peace—took her in and became her father. You would be surprised how many times people leave babies at circuses,” Arthur didn’t seem to mind the wet horse taste of his cigarettes.

Detective Wentworth continued to take notes; Father: Deceased. Mother: Unknown. No next of kin. “Doeth thhe have a boyfriend?” he then asked.

“No. There was this fling she had with one of the clowns—Thomas—recently, but that was quite short-lived. I’ve, of course, already had a word with him: Says he’s fine and that life goes on.”

“And you believe him?”

“Undoubtedly. He’s always been more like a brother to her anyway, stepping in and defending her when audience members would get too friendly.”

“Are there any other people I thhould talk to?” Detective Wentworth asked before putting out the cigarette and getting up to leave the wagon.

By the time he had talked to and crossing out all but one name from the scrabbled down list in his newspaper-turned-notebook, the sun had already set and it was getting dark. After much searching Detective Wentworth had finally found Thomas behind the lion cage as he was getting ready for tonight’s show.

Thomas, who had made no efforts to hide his distaste for Detective Wentworth’s presence had also appeared eager to get rid of him by excusing himself with needing to get ready for the show of the night, “I’m sorry Mr. Wentworth but Samuel doesn’t like it when we’re late,” he had said before leaving.

“Whatever the clown ith hiding he’th doing it well. I thuppothe thpending motht of your life behind a mathk will do that to you,” Detective Wentworth thought to himself as he underlined Thomas’ name on his makeshift list.

Standing there he could hear thunder in the distance and with the southbound winds tonight it wouldn’t take long for “God’s Wrath”—as his father would call it—to rain down on the departing visitors of the Sparrow Brothers and their “Most Expectacular Show on Earth” circus.

“What ith he hiding? Doeth he know where thith Minnie girl ith? He thtriketh me ath a man hiding a debt more than kidnapping a young woman, but then again, what do I know?” Detective Wentworth thought to himself as he tried to light a cigarette in the increasing wind, “I’ll thhadow you after the thhow, thooner or later you’ll make a mithtake. Everyone maketh mithtaketh.”

And just like Detective Wentworth had anticipated, a few hours later the rain was already pouring down fast and hard and the looming thunderstorms painted the night sky in shades of black and grey. He had patiently waited for the evening’s show to end and for Thomas—the punctual clown—to let down his guard.

After the show Thomas and few circus workers had hitched a ride into nearby ‘Mound City’ where they had proceeded to drink and hustle the locals.

“Everyone maketh mithtakes,” Detective Wentworth reminded himself as the pouring rain was filling his shoes with the mud from the alley in which he was standing.

But just as Detective Wentworth was about to give up, conceding to being the one who had made the mistake, he saw Thomas coming out from the Butler’s Arm, alone.

Shadowing him proved to be easier than Detective Wentworth had expected as Thomas in his tipsy state of mind didn’t suspect that he was being followed as he made his way straight to a shadier part of ‘Mound City’.

The dim candle light couldn’t conceal how worn down the apartment she was hiding in was. The wallpapers were peeling themselves of the walls, as if to try to escape their inevitable fate, and the air tasted like mold.

“Mth. Walker. You’ve been miththed,” standing drenched in the alley had given Detective Wentworth plenty of time to think about what he would say and he was feeling quite proud of this particular line.

“You don’t understand. I have to—”

“Thave yourthelf the explanation. I don’t need to underthtand. I wath hired to find you and that’th what I did. I’m jutht doing my job, that’th all I do,” Detective Wentworth made sure he was still armed by running his finger across the cold steel of the M1917 Revolver in his right pocket.

“You Pinkertons are all the same. Messin’ up things you don’t understand. Well you’re going to have to go through me first,” puffing himself up Thomas tried to look intimidating.

Detective Wentworth remained calm whilst pulling out his revolver and aiming it at Thomas, “Look, I’m no Pinkerton but that doethn’t mean I won’t uthe thith. Let’th jutht thave ourthelveth the trouble.”

“He’ll beat her black and blue. You can’t do this to her, not when she’s having a—”

“Sssh Thomas, he won’t care about that,” interrupting Thomas, Minnie instinctively put her hand to her stomach.

Detective Wentworth looked at Minnie for a moment before asking her with a warmth he hadn’t felt since his days with Josephine, “Are… you?”.

“I am,” Minnie smiled cautiously, stroking her stomach as she spoke, “2 months now and I’m afraid that it’s going to start showing soon,” her voice turned cold, “But they won’t allow it.”

For a moment they all stood there and the only sound that could be heard was the angry rain beating down on the roof above them.

“Juth go. Get out of the thity. Get ath far away ath poththible,” Detective Wentworth spoke with a firm but soft voice.

Swinging out the cylinder he unloaded the revolver, handed it and its bullets to her, “Here, take the revolver. There’th a lot of bad people out there, you’ll need thith to keep you thafe.”

“Thank you, thank you, thank you. I don’t know what to say,” Minnie nervously put the revolver in her trunk.

Thomas, still distrustful and dumbfounded by Detective Wentworth’s sudden change of heart, but not wanting to appear unhelpful added, “When I get back to the circus I’ll distract them to buy you some extra time.”

“Thank you Thomas, you’ve always been a good friend,” turning to Detective Wentworth Minnie continued, “Again, thank you. I didn’t even catch your name.”

“Detective Rithhard William Wentworth at your thervice, Ma’am,” he said trying to lighten the mood but his voice wouldn’t obey.

“William,” Minnie paused, picking up her trunk, “I like that. But you should consider a different profession before this one gets the best of you.”

A few days later, instead of reporting back to Arthur Sparrow, Detective Wentworth took the earliest train he could back to Chicago.

He later read in the newspaper about an incident at a traveling circus in Missouri where one of the owners had snapped over a disagreement and beaten an employee to death. The newspaper had only referred to Thomas as an unnamed clown with no next of kin, in what they had penned as a horrible display of human brutality.

Detective Wentworth had exchanged his glass of water for a glass of bad scotch, “That’th it. I’m finithhed being a detective.”

 

Fin.
